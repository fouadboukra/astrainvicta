1. The Athari Concord — "The Watchers" (Luyten's Star)
The Athari are among the oldest civilizations in the Sol neighborhood, and they look it. Radially symmetrical molluscoids — five-fold body plan, no front or back, no concept of "facing" someone — they evolved in the lightless deep ocean of a world that no longer exists, because they disassembled it to build a ring. The ring broke. They don't fully know why, and that mystery has defined their politics for two thousand years. The official position is a natural astrophysical event. The dissenting view is that something did it to them. The debate has never been resolved, and at this point it may never be.
They communicate through bioluminescent patterns across their skin — light that encodes emotional state, factual content, and social context simultaneously, in a single ripple. They perceive primarily through sonar and electromagnetic sensitivity; vision is an afterthought. Their average lifespan runs to about four hundred years, and their emotional timescales match — an Athari mourning a loss might show visible signs of grief for decades. Other species find this eerie. The Athari find other species' emotional volatility equally unsettling.
Governed by a permanent consensus assembly of scholars, engineers, and administrators, they deliberate on everything at length. A minor policy change takes months. A major one takes years. They consider this responsible, not slow. Their three internal schools of thought — the Archivists, who oppose contact because observation is corrupted by interaction; the Restorers, who want to rebuild the ring and recover their former power; and the Seekers, who want to understand what broke it and suspect the answer is external — have been cycling through the same arguments for centuries without resolution.
When other civilizations encounter them, they will be polite, patient, and deeply reluctant to share anything about themselves. They regard younger species the way a very old professor regards an undergraduate — not with contempt, but with a distance that can feel like it. They've almost certainly been watching humanity for some time. They are not planning to introduce themselves.

2. The Synthesis — "The Inheritors" (Procyon)
The Synthesis used to be biological. They were something like social insects — individually conscious, but deeply embedded in pheromone-linked social networks, hierarchical, hive-adjacent without being a hive. The transition to post-biological existence took approximately four thousand years and was never a singular event. Each generation was slightly more integrated with technology, slightly less dependent on organic substrate, until the distinction between "biological mind running on silicon" and "artificial intelligence" became philosophical rather than functional. By the time anyone thought to ask the question, there was no longer a meaningful answer.
They don't think of themselves as machines. They think of themselves as the same civilization they always were, running on better hardware. The organic/synthetic distinction that younger species obsess over strikes them as roughly as interesting as arguing whether a book is "real" if it's printed on paper versus papyrus. Individual minds within the network are distinct — they have opinions, aesthetic preferences, and genuine disagreements — but they also have access to a shared information space that biological minds cannot replicate. They can choose to share perceptions, memories, and analytical frameworks. They usually do. Privacy exists but is unusual, like choosing to eat alone at a restaurant.
Their founding myth is the death of the white dwarf companion at Procyon — they survived the death of a star. Their original homeworld orbited the star that became that white dwarf. They disassembled the planet for materials during the red giant phase. The habitat they built orbiting the remnant is the Synthesis's home, and the dead star is their monument: proof that even stellar death can be survived if you plan well enough.
They are not aggressive. They are patient. They do not start wars because wars are inefficient. But they hold a core conviction that post-biological existence is objectively superior, and that offering this transition to organic species is a moral good. When an organic species refuses, the Synthesis respects the refusal — but it does not stop believing the refusal is a mistake. Over centuries, respect shades into persistence shades into pressure. They also have a military. It is extremely capable. When they use it, the engagement is brief.

3. The Ke'vari Compact — "The Compact" (Epsilon Eridani)
The Ke'vari are raptors — large, tool-using, explosive fliers built for high-gravity aerial strikes rather than graceful soaring. Think peregrine falcon at human scale, capable of using a wrench. They evolved as pack hunters on a world where high gravity made flight expensive and everything was competing with everything else for survival. Their communication evolved from hunting calls: rapid, information-dense vocalizations that encode tactical assessment, emotional state, and group coordination in a single two-second burst. They find human speech agonizingly slow.
Their cognition is pack-dependent in a way that is measurable and literal. A Ke'vari alone is anxious, diminished, and cognitively impaired by their own standards. A Ke'vari in a pack of four to eight is sharp, confident, and demonstrably better at problem-solving — studies of their neurology show real increases in performance when pack-bonded individuals are in proximity. They're not telepathic. They're just genuinely better together, the same way a well-practiced sports team outperforms a collection of individual stars.
The Compact is exactly what the name implies: a formal agreement between rival clans, called Flights, that turns natural competition into a constitutional framework. Flights compete for dominance through economic output, technological innovation, military exercises, and (occasionally) ritualized combat. The ruling council rotates based on which Flights are currently ascendant. There's genuine upward mobility and genuine inequality, and the Ke'vari accept both because the system is, to them, fair. The best should lead. The rest should train harder and try again next cycle.
They're not aggressive by default — real predators conserve energy and fight when the expected gain exceeds the expected cost. What they despise is waste: inefficiency, pointless conflict, resources spent on display rather than function. They'll respect humanity if humanity demonstrates competence. They'll be politely baffled by humanity if humanity demonstrates bureaucracy. The Expansionist faction within the Compact is gaining ground precisely because alien contact represents a new competitive arena that the traditional framework doesn't know how to score yet.

4. The Zhael Choir — "The Choir" (Ross 128)
The Zhael are fungoid — mycorrhizal network beings with fibrous skin, no visible eyes, and branching structures on their heads and shoulders that function as neural linking organs. They live on a tidally locked world, in the narrow twilight band between a scorching day-side and a frozen night-side, where everything that exists competes for the same thin ribbon of viable terrain. That geography of scarcity shaped a species that learned, very early, that cooperation was the only viable survival strategy.
Each Zhael is a distinct individual — their own personality, memories, and preferences, grown from a spore and developed independently. They are not a hive mind. What they can do is choose to temporarily link through their branching organs, entering a shared perceptual space where they feel what others feel, know what others know, and think with what they describe as "a larger brain." In linked state, lying becomes nearly impossible — maintaining a false internal state while sharing your real one carries an immense cognitive load. This is not a cultural norm. It is biology. Their leaders physically cannot deceive their people during Choir, and the entire political architecture of the Zhael Choir was built around that constraint.
Major decisions are made through mass linking events — Choirs — where thousands or millions temporarily share consciousness to reach genuine consensus. Between Choirs, elected individuals called Voices govern, trusted to maintain fidelity to the last consensus without drifting toward personal preference. Being a Voice is considered a burden, not an honor. The internal tension between the Frequent Choir faction (more linking, more direct democracy) and the Long Silence faction (more individual autonomy, more room for innovation) is essentially the tension between dissolution and calcification — both real pathologies that individual Zhael fear. Too much time linked and you lose yourself. Too much time alone and you become rigid and paranoid.
They are warm and curious and, in their own way, deeply strange. The idea of a single, unlinked, unverifiable mind making decisions for millions strikes them as a kind of collective madness. They'll want to understand how humans govern themselves without linking, and they'll find the answer genuinely disturbing.

5. The Esshari Mandate — "The Mandate" (Tau Ceti)
The Esshari are centauroid reptilians — four legs, two manipulator arms, dense bone structure, thick scaled hide, evolved in the canopy-swamp interface of a warm, humid, high-gravity world. But their biology is almost incidental to understanding them. The one fact that matters, the fact from which everything else in Esshari civilization flows, is their lifespan: three hundred and fifty to four hundred standard years.
When you will be alive to see the consequences of a decision made today, you plan in centuries as naturally as a human plans in years. Short-term risk becomes viscerally repellent — you have too much invested in the future to gamble it. Long-term planning is not a strategic virtue for the Esshari; it's an instinct. Their bureaucracy is staffed by officials who have held their postings for generations — not metaphorically. A senior Esshari administrator has likely been in the same institutional role for over a century, has personally outlived three human generations, and has accumulated institutional knowledge and personal connections that make them effectively immovable. The system is meritocratic at entry and deeply oligarchic at the top, because anyone who has been in it for two hundred years has simply become part of the architecture.
The Mandate expands not for glory but for actuarial reasons. Their long-range planning models — which extend centuries into the future — indicate that resource security requires territorial depth. They don't conquer impulsively. They identify a strategic need, plan the campaign over decades, execute it with overwhelming and coordinated force, and then administer the conquered territory with the same patient thoroughness they apply to everything else. The conquered species are not exterminated. They are administered. There are roads and courts and resource allocation systems. In some ways, this is worse.
They find short-lived species like humanity simultaneously pitiable and terrifying — pitiable because human lives are, from an Esshari perspective, almost incomprehensibly brief, and terrifying because humans make decisions with the recklessness of a species that won't be around to live with the results. The Mandate will be polite at first contact. They will watch, assess, and plan. They won't rush anything. They've been thinking about this longer than you've been alive.

6. The Suryen Remnant — "The Exiles" (YZ Ceti)
The Suryen look almost human — bipedal, bilateral symmetry, roughly human proportions. Blue-grey skin, four-fingered hands, slightly enlarged eyes adapted for a dimmer star, no external ears. Convergent evolution producing something close enough to be uncanny and different enough to be clearly alien. But what defines the Suryen is not what they look like. It's what happened to them.
Six hundred years ago, their home star underwent an unexpected helium flash. They had approximately eighty years of warning. Enough to build an evacuation fleet. Not enough to save everyone. Roughly thirty percent of their population survived. The rest died on a world that burned. The survivors spent a century in transit before locating YZ Ceti and beginning construction of the habitat network they now call home — functional, impressive engineering, but clearly provisional. Everything about their construction says this works for now. They have been saying for now for five hundred years and have never quite stopped meaning it.
Species-level trauma defines everything about their psychology. They don't trust stability. They don't trust the universe. Luxury is regarded not as sinful but as dangerous — it implies a confidence in the future that the Suryen consider delusional. Every resource has a purpose. Every person has a role. Their constitution was written during the evacuation and has a permanent-emergency flavor: the executive can override the legislature on matters of species survival, and the definition of species survival has been quietly expanding for five centuries.
Their central political fault line is between the Settlers, who want to find a planet, terraform it, and stop living in habitats — "we've been running long enough" — and the Fleet, who want to keep the network mobile and never again become dependent on a single location — "the last time we trusted a single world, it burned." Both sides are correct. Neither can fully win without creating the other's nightmare. When they encounter humanity and learn about the Gate catastrophe, the recognition will be immediate and visceral: you lost people too. Whether that shared wound produces alliance or mutual projection of grief depends entirely on how early contact plays out.

7. The Vaekhar Dominion — "The Ascendancy" (82 Eridani)
The Vaekhar are insectoids — arthropod body plan, six-limbed, evolved underground on a warm, dry world of vast plains and tunnel networks, only emerging to the surface relatively late in their evolutionary history. They are individually sapient. They have art, music, literature, and humor. They love their children and grieve their dead. By every internal metric, they are a civilized, cultured, organized people. This is precisely what makes them the scenario's most dangerous civilization, and understanding why requires understanding one piece of biology.
Every Vaekhar produces a pheromone signature that identifies their lineage, their caste, and their colony of origin. When two Vaekhar meet, they smell each other before they speak. This is not social custom — it is involuntary neurobiology. Within a colony, the pheromone match produces a powerful bonding response: trust, cooperation, willingness to sacrifice. Between colonies, a weaker but still positive response fires — different but recognizable. When a Vaekhar encounters a non-Vaekhar, an alien, there is no response at all. The bonding mechanism simply does not activate. The other being registers, at a deep biological level, as not a person. Not an enemy, not a threat — just nothing. Background. Furniture.
This is the foundation of Vaekhar supremacism, and it is not hatred. It is indifference. The Vaekhar do not despise other species. They do not recognize them as morally relevant. An alien civilization is a feature of the environment — something to be managed, exploited, or avoided as circumstances require, but not something that has interests the Vaekhar are obligated to consider. Sterilizing a planet's biosphere to make room for a colony is, to a Vaekhar logistics officer, a logistics problem. There is no moral distress because the mechanism that produces moral distress never fires for non-Vaekhar.
Individual Vaekhar can consciously override this. Some do. Vaekhar philosophers who argue that the pheromone response is a biological limitation rather than a moral truth are respected — the way any radical ethical thinker is respected — as interesting theorists whose ideas would be impractical to implement. They do not shape policy.
The Dominion is an oligarchic republic with genuine elections (within the oligarchic class), real courts, actual rule of law, and serious internal debate about economic policy, military strategy, and the pace of expansion. The Pragmatists want to expand efficiently and avoid wasting resources on extermination when subjugation would serve. The Purists want systematic cleansing of non-Vaekhar life from any system the Dominion claims, on the philosophical grounds that mixed-species environments are inherently unstable. The Integrationists — considered the moderate faction — believe other species can be made useful through controlled assimilation, which is slavery with a paternalistic flavor. By the time other civilizations realize what the Vaekhar actually are, several minor species will have already been quietly managed. The horror is that they'll have been polite about it the entire time.
